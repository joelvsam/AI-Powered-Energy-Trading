# BEGINNER TO EXPERT GUIDE
# AI-Powered Energy Trading Repository

========================================================================
1. WHAT THIS PROJECT IS
========================================================================

Let us start with the simplest possible explanation.

This repository is a research system for energy trading.

That means it is a serious environment for asking a hard question:

"If we use data, forecasting, and trading rules, can we find signals that
might make money in electricity markets after realistic trading frictions?"

That is a very different question from:

"Can we predict prices reasonably well?"

And it is also different from:

"Can we build a fully automated production trading machine?"

This project sits in the middle.

It is more serious than a classroom machine learning demo.
It is less complete than an institutional live trading system.

It is designed to help a researcher, junior quant, data scientist, or
curious learner understand whether a trading idea has believable edge.

You can think of it like a wind tunnel for airplanes.

A wind tunnel is not the real sky.
It does not replace flying a real aircraft.
But it is much better than guessing.

In the same way:

- this repository is not the live market
- it is not an auto-trader sending orders to exchanges
- it is not a full risk engine used by a major trading firm
- but it is a disciplined environment for testing trading ideas honestly

What does the repository actually do?

At a high level, it does this:

1. Collect market and weather data.
2. Reuse validated raw-data cache history and fetch only missing ranges.
3. Clean the data.
4. Create useful features from the raw data.
5. Train forecasting models.
6. Turn forecasts and structural market information into trading signals.
7. Simulate trading with realistic frictions like spread and slippage.
8. Compare the strategy against strong simple baselines.
9. Check whether outperformance looks statistically meaningful.
10. Produce a research-style note explaining what worked, what failed, and why.

That last part matters a lot.

This repository does not just ask:

"Did the strategy make money?"

It also asks:

"Did it beat easy alternatives?"
"Did it survive costs?"
"Did it work only in one lucky period?"
"Do we understand why it works?"
"Is this really signal, or just noise?"

That mindset is the difference between a normal machine learning project
and a research-grade trading project.

What is energy trading, and why does it exist?

Energy trading exists because electricity must be produced and consumed in
real time, and the world is messy.

People switch lights on and off.
Factories ramp up.
Weather changes.
Wind farms produce more or less power.
Solar output rises and falls with clouds and time of day.
Power plants fail unexpectedly.
Transmission lines have limits.

All of that creates uncertainty.

If you are responsible for supplying electricity, consuming electricity,
balancing a grid, or managing exposure to power prices, you care deeply
about what might happen next.

So traders, utilities, grid operators, renewable producers, industrial
consumers, and market makers all use markets to transfer risk and to
balance mismatches.

Why are electricity markets special?

Electricity is unlike oil, wheat, or shoes.

If you buy shoes, you can store them in a warehouse.
If you buy wheat, you can put it in a silo.
If supply is too high today, maybe you store it and sell later.

Electricity is much harder.

Large-scale storage exists, but it is limited and expensive relative to the
size of the market.

So power markets behave more like a giant balancing act than a normal goods
market.

A useful analogy:

Imagine a giant seesaw carrying the entire economy.
On one side is electricity supply.
On the other side is electricity demand.

The seesaw must remain balanced almost every second.

If supply suddenly falls or demand suddenly jumps, the system cannot calmly
wait until tomorrow. It must react now.

That is why electricity prices can move violently.

Why does renewable energy increase volatility?

Renewables like wind and solar are valuable, but they are weather-driven.

If wind speeds drop, wind generation can fall quickly.
If clouds arrive, solar generation can decline fast.
If forecasts were wrong, traders and operators must suddenly rebalance.

That creates short-term price stress.

This is one reason power markets can be more volatile than a beginner
expects.

Why does forecasting matter?

Because if you can estimate future demand, renewable output, price stress,
or imbalance risk even slightly better than others, you may gain an edge.

But forecasting alone is not enough.

This is one of the most important lessons in trading research.

A forecast can be accurate and still be useless for trading.

Why?

Because trading has costs and constraints.

Suppose your model predicts that price will rise by EUR 0.20 per MWh.
That sounds directionally correct.
But if the spread, slippage, and costs together are EUR 0.40 per MWh,
your "good forecast" loses money in practice.

This is why trading systems differ from normal ML projects.

A normal ML project may focus on:

- prediction accuracy
- RMSE
- MAE
- classification accuracy

A trading research project must also care about:

- tradability
- execution timing
- transaction costs
- liquidity
- risk-adjusted returns
- drawdowns
- robustness across regimes
- comparison against naive benchmarks

In other words:

A normal ML project asks, "Can I predict?"
A trading research project asks, "Can I predict something that is useful,
robust, and profitable after friction?"

What does "research-grade" mean in this repository?

It does not mean "institutional production ready."

It means the repository tries to behave like serious research rather than
marketing theater.

In this repository, "research-grade" means:

- canonical raw-data caches for market and weather history rather than
  refetching the entire window every run
- cache validation with schema checks, duplicate protection, timezone
  consistency, and atomic writes
- row-level provenance showing whether each raw row is real, partially
  synthetic, or fully synthetic
- walk-forward validation instead of naive random splits
- automatic adjustment of the train window when feature engineering trims the
  usable sample but still leaves enough history for honest walk-forward testing
- realistic execution delay
- spread and slippage modeling
- position caps and liquidity limits
- baseline comparisons
- bootstrap significance testing
- model diagnostics
- graceful fallback when optional ENTSO-E intraday or imbalance context is
  unavailable
- explicit marking of synthetic data runs as non-research-grade
- honest reporting of limitations

Why cache raw data at all?

Because repeated research runs often overlap heavily in history.

If you rerun a 180-day study today and then rerun a 365-day study tomorrow,
most of the older timestamps are the same observations.

Re-downloading or regenerating that entire history every time wastes time and
creates unnecessary operational fragility.

But a serious cache must be careful.

It cannot behave like a casual performance shortcut.

It must behave like a market-data persistence layer.

That means:

- only raw market and weather data are cached
- engineered features are not cached
- predictions are not cached
- backtests are not cached
- cache writes are validated before they replace the previous file
- synthetic rows stay visible instead of pretending to be real history

Why cache only raw data?

Because raw data is the stable upstream input.

Features, signals, predictions, and backtests depend on modeling choices.
If you cached those layers, you could accidentally freeze old research logic
and create stale results that look current.

Caching raw data speeds up reruns without bypassing the honest recomputation
of everything downstream.

Why provenance matters:

Suppose an intraday endpoint fails for 8 hours in the middle of your sample.

If the system silently fills those rows and forgets about it, you may treat
those periods as equally trustworthy to fully real market data.

That is dangerous.

So every cached raw row carries a truth label:

- real
- partially synthetic
- synthetic

This keeps the research note, dashboard, and analyst aware of data quality
instead of hiding it behind a smooth dataframe.

Why partial synthetic fallback is better than replacing the whole dataset:

If 95% of a historical window is real and 5% is unavailable, throwing away the
real 95% would be wasteful and less honest than preserving it.

A better design is:

- keep the real rows
- fetch only missing timestamp ranges
- synthesize only unresolved rows or fields
- report exactly how much contamination remains

This preserves useful history while still respecting uncertainty.

How leakage risk is avoided in the cache:

The cache stores rows by timestamp and source provenance.
It does not store future-aware engineered features.
It does not let synthetic rows overwrite better real rows.
It does not recompute old history from future signals.
And it only refreshes the requested raw window unless a full rebuild is asked
for explicitly.

That honesty matters.

Many trading projects look exciting because they skip realism.

They may:

- use future information by accident
- ignore costs
- assume instant execution
- compare against weak baselines
- present one lucky backtest as proof

This repository tries not to do that.

Still, it is important to be very clear:

This repository is NOT a production auto-trader.

It does not:

- place real orders
- manage a live portfolio across many markets
- maintain exchange connectivity
- run a full institutional risk engine
- model the full order book
- solve all latency and infrastructure problems

So what is it?

It is a structured research lab for testing whether predictive and
structural signals may have tradable edge after more honest assumptions.


========================================================================
2. ENERGY TRADING FOR COMPLETE BEGINNERS
========================================================================

This section assumes you know almost nothing about power markets.

That is fine.

We will build the intuition slowly.

What is a power grid?

A power grid is the system that moves electricity from producers to users.

It includes:

- power plants
- wind farms
- solar farms
- transmission lines
- substations
- distribution networks
- homes
- factories
- offices

The grid is like a giant highway system, except instead of cars moving, it
is electricity flowing.

What makes it tricky is that the "traffic" must stay balanced.

What is balancing?

Balancing means total electricity supply must match total electricity demand
at nearly every moment.

If too much power is produced, system stability is threatened.
If too little power is produced, the same thing happens.

Imagine a bathtub where water must stay at exactly the right level.

- Too much inflow: overflow.
- Too little inflow: water level drops dangerously.

The grid works similarly, except the consequences can involve frequency
instability, emergency actions, or in extreme cases outages.

Who are generators?

Generators are entities that produce electricity.

Examples:

- gas plants
- coal plants
- nuclear plants
- hydro plants
- wind farms
- solar farms

Who are consumers?

Consumers are the users of electricity.

Examples:

- households
- factories
- data centers
- rail systems
- office buildings

Why is renewable intermittency important?

"Intermittent" means output is not fully controllable.

If you own a gas plant, you can often decide whether to ramp generation up
or down.

If you own a wind farm, you cannot command the wind to blow.
If you own solar panels, you cannot command the sun to shine through clouds.

So renewable generation is partly forecastable, but not perfectly
controllable.

This matters because forecast errors create balancing stress.

Why do prices spike?

Power prices can spike because the system has to balance immediately, and
the last available unit of supply may be expensive.

Imagine 99% of demand can be met cheaply, but the last 1% requires fast,
scarce, expensive generation.

Then a small shortage can push prices sharply higher.

Now let us explain the main market layers.

-----------------------------
DAY-AHEAD MARKET
-----------------------------

Definition:

The day-ahead market is where participants buy and sell electricity for
delivery tomorrow, usually hour by hour.

Why does it exist?

Because the grid needs a forward plan.

Generators want to know how much to produce tomorrow.
Consumers and utilities want to lock in expected needs.
Renewable producers want to schedule expected generation.

Who uses it?

- utilities
- generators
- industrial consumers
- suppliers
- traders

How do traders use it?

They use it to express views about tomorrow's supply-demand balance, weather
impact, fuel relationships, and relative value across hours or regions.

Simple example:

Suppose tomorrow morning is expected to be cold.
Heating demand may increase.
A trader may expect higher day-ahead prices in morning hours and position
accordingly.

Analogy:

The day-ahead market is like booking tomorrow's airline seats in advance.
You are planning ahead based on what you think demand and capacity will be.

-----------------------------
INTRADAY MARKET
-----------------------------

Definition:

The intraday market is where participants update positions closer to real
time after new information arrives.

Why does it exist?

Because tomorrow's forecast is never perfect.

Weather changes.
Power plants trip offline.
Demand surprises happen.
Renewable forecasts get updated.

Who uses it?

- traders
- utilities
- renewable producers
- balance responsible parties
- market makers

How do traders use it?

They use it to react to forecast changes, hedge exposures, exploit short-term
mispricing, and manage delivery risk.

Practical example:

At 10:00 AM today, tomorrow's solar forecast looks strong.
At 4:00 PM, new weather data shows heavy cloud cover tomorrow afternoon.
Intraday prices for those hours may move because expected solar supply fell.

Analogy:

If day-ahead is planning your trip yesterday, intraday is making same-day
adjustments when you discover traffic, weather, or flight delays.

-----------------------------
IMBALANCE MARKET
-----------------------------

Definition:

The imbalance market handles the difference between what participants
scheduled and what actually happens in real time.

Why does it exist?

Because reality never matches plans perfectly.

If a participant promised one amount of power but actually delivers or
consumes another amount, the system operator must correct the mismatch.

That correction has a price.

Who uses it?

- balance responsible parties
- utilities
- generators
- renewable producers
- traders managing delivery risk

How do traders think about it?

They care because imbalance prices can become very high or very low when the
system is stressed.

If you can forecast imbalance stress better than others, you may manage risk
better or find opportunity.

Real-world style example:

A wind forecast was too optimistic.
Actual wind generation is much lower than expected.
The system needs expensive balancing energy quickly.
Imbalance prices jump.

Analogy:

Imagine ordering food for 100 guests but only 70 portions arrive.
At the last minute, you must buy emergency replacements from the nearest
open vendor, probably at terrible prices.

That is the spirit of imbalance pricing.

-----------------------------
ANCILLARY SERVICES
-----------------------------

Definition:

Ancillary services are supporting services that help keep the power system
stable, such as reserves and frequency control.

Why do they exist?

Because the grid needs more than just average supply and demand matching.
It also needs stability, frequency support, reserve capacity, and emergency
response.

Who uses them?

- system operators
- generators
- storage providers
- flexible demand providers

How can traders care?

Even if a trader is not directly trading ancillary products, ancillary system
stress can affect short-term price formation and the value of flexible assets.

Analogy:

If the main market is the public road, ancillary services are the tow trucks,
traffic police, and emergency lanes that keep everything functional when
something goes wrong.

-----------------------------
MARKET COUPLING
-----------------------------

Definition:

Market coupling is the linking of electricity markets across regions so that
cross-border transmission capacity and price formation can be coordinated.

Why does it exist?

Because electricity does not care about national borders in the same way
politics does. If one region has cheaper supply and there is transmission
capacity available, power can flow across borders.

Who uses it?

- exchanges
- system operators
- cross-border traders
- utilities

Why does it matter to traders?

Because prices in one country can affect prices in another.
Transmission limits can cause price separation.
Cross-border constraints can create dislocations and local scarcity.

Analogy:

Imagine neighboring cities connected by bridges.
If one city has cheap food and the other city is short, goods can move across
the bridge until the bridge is full.
Once the bridge reaches capacity, prices may split.

Why are European power markets difficult?

Because many moving parts interact at once:

- weather
- renewables
- cross-border flows
- fuel economics
- outages
- regulation
- transmission limits
- market design details

This creates a rich but difficult environment for modeling.

Why does weather matter so much?

Because weather affects both demand and supply.

Examples:

- cold weather can raise heating demand
- hot weather can raise cooling demand
- wind affects wind generation
- clouds affect solar generation
- temperature can also affect transmission and consumption patterns

Why do renewable forecasts matter?

Because renewable output changes the net amount of conventional generation
needed by the system.

If renewable supply is high, prices may soften.
If renewable supply underperforms expectations, prices may jump.

Why can imbalance prices explode?

Because imbalance pricing often reflects the cost of emergency correction in
real time.

If the system is short and fast-response energy is scarce, the price of fixing
the problem can be very high.

This is one reason short-term energy trading can be both attractive and
dangerous.


========================================================================
3. DATA SCIENCE INSIDE AN ENERGY TRADING FIRM
========================================================================

What does a data scientist do at an energy trading desk?

Many beginners imagine a data scientist simply builds a prediction model and
hands it off.

In reality, the job is broader.

A data scientist in power trading may work on:

- forecasting demand
- forecasting renewable generation
- forecasting short-term prices
- detecting bad data or unusual events
- constructing signals
- measuring whether signals produce tradable edge
- helping with execution assumptions
- helping risk teams understand exposures
- explaining model behavior to traders

Let us explain these carefully.

Forecasting demand:

This means estimating future electricity consumption.

Why does it matter?

Because if demand will be much higher than expected, prices may rise.

Forecasting renewables:

This means estimating wind or solar output.

Why does it matter?

Because renewables can displace expensive generation when abundant, or create
scarcity when they underperform.

Forecasting prices:

This means estimating future power prices directly.

Why does it matter?

Because traders care about where prices may move.

Detecting anomalies:

This means spotting strange or suspicious data behavior:

- missing values
- extreme spikes
- structural breaks
- weird patterns from bad ingestion or regime shifts

Why does anomaly detection matter?

Because trading models built on broken data often produce fake confidence and
bad decisions.

Optimizing positions:

This means deciding how large a long or short exposure should be, not merely
whether the price may go up or down.

Risk management:

This means controlling how much can be lost, how concentrated exposure
becomes, and how fragile performance is during stress.

Execution constraints:

This means modeling the reality that you cannot always get the exact trade you
want at the exact price you want instantly.

Now let us explain one of the deepest ideas in trading research:

Prediction accuracy is not the same as profitability.

Suppose Model A has slightly better RMSE than Model B.

That does not guarantee Model A is better for trading.

Why not?

Because the errors that matter for trading are not always the same errors that
matter for average prediction metrics.

If Model A is good on calm periods but bad during the few high-impact stressed
hours, it may look statistically fine but trade badly.

If Model B captures big dislocations better, it may have worse average error
but better trading performance.

This is why low RMSE can still lose money.

What is alpha?

Simple definition:

Alpha means a useful edge.

Technical definition:

Alpha is the component of performance that comes from predictive or structural
skill rather than pure market exposure or luck.

Why it matters:

Without alpha, you do not really have a strategy. You have noise wearing a
nice suit.

What is a signal?

Simple definition:

A signal is a clue that suggests what the market may do.

Technical definition:

A signal is a numeric or logical representation of information used to guide
trading decisions.

Why it matters:

Signals are the bridge between data and action.

What is Sharpe ratio?

Simple definition:

Sharpe ratio is a way to ask:

"How much reward did I get for the risk I took?"

Technical definition:

Sharpe ratio is the average return divided by return volatility, often
annualized.

Why it matters:

A strategy that makes money with wild instability may be less attractive than
a steadier one.

What is turnover?

Simple definition:

Turnover means how much you keep changing your position.

If you buy, then sell, then buy, then sell constantly, turnover is high.

Why it matters:

High turnover often means more costs.

What is drawdown?

Simple definition:

Drawdown is how far your strategy falls from a previous high.

If your account grows from 100 to 120, then falls to 90, you experienced a
big drawdown.

Why it matters:

Even profitable strategies can be unacceptable if they suffer brutal
drawdowns.

What is overfitting?

Simple definition:

Overfitting means your model memorizes the past instead of learning a useful
pattern.

Analogy:

It is like a student who memorizes yesterday's test answers but cannot solve
new questions.

Why it matters:

An overfit model often looks brilliant in backtests and disappointing in real
life.

What is leakage?

Simple definition:

Leakage means your model accidentally sees information from the future while
pretending to predict the future.

Analogy:

It is like taking an exam while secretly reading the answer key.

Why it matters:

Leakage can create completely fake trading results.

What is walk-forward validation?

Simple definition:

Train on the past, test on the next unseen chunk, then move forward and
repeat.

Why it matters:

This matches the real life logic of trading much better than random data
splits.

What is regime switching?

Simple definition:

Regime switching means markets do not always behave the same way.

Sometimes trends dominate.
Sometimes mean reversion dominates.
Sometimes volatility is calm.
Sometimes volatility is explosive.

Why it matters:

A strategy that ignores regime changes may use the wrong behavior at the
wrong time.

What is volatility scaling?

Simple definition:

Volatility scaling means reducing position size when the market becomes more
violent.

Analogy:

If the road is icy, you drive more carefully.

Why it matters:

High volatility can amplify losses and worsen execution.

What is execution delay?

Simple definition:

Execution delay means you cannot act at the exact instant the signal is
computed.

Why it matters:

Real traders receive information, make decisions, route orders, and get
filled with delay. Ignoring that can create fake edge.

What is slippage?

Simple definition:

Slippage is the loss from getting a worse price than the price you hoped for.

Why it matters:

In volatile or illiquid markets, slippage can destroy apparently good trades.

What is latency?

Simple definition:

Latency is delay in information processing, systems, or execution.

Why it matters:

In fast markets, being late means paying up.

What is a market regime?

Simple definition:

A market regime is the "mood" or behavior state of the market.

Examples:

- calm
- volatile
- trending
- mean-reverting

Why it matters:

The same signal can behave differently in different regimes.


========================================================================
4. REPOSITORY WALKTHROUGH
========================================================================

Now we will walk through the architecture.

The goal is not just to know file names, but to understand why each part
exists.

-----------------------------
src/config.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

This file centralizes configuration.

It defines the AppConfig object, which stores settings like:

- directories
- default zone
- lookback window
- transaction costs
- signal settings
- execution settings
- bootstrap settings
- LLM options

Why it exists:

Central configuration keeps the project organized.

Without it, settings would be scattered across many files, which makes the
system harder to reason about and easier to break.

Inputs:

- environment variables
- code defaults

Outputs:

- a consistent configuration object used across the pipeline

Why this design was chosen:

A dataclass-based configuration is readable, explicit, and easy for a junior
researcher to inspect.

Alternative:

- YAML-only configuration
- command-line-only configuration
- many global constants

Tradeoff:

The current pattern is simple and clear, but large institutional systems often
need more sophisticated configuration management.

-----------------------------
src/data_sources/entsoe_client.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

This file talks to ENTSO-E and normalizes returned market data.

It fetches:

- day-ahead prices
- load forecasts
- renewable generation
- intraday prices
- imbalance prices
- intraday renewable forecast

Why it exists:

Raw market APIs are messy.

This module isolates the complexity of:

- querying endpoints
- chunking long time ranges
- converting responses into clean tables
- handling different shapes of ENTSO-E data

Inputs:

- API key
- zone
- start/end timestamps
- timeout and chunk parameters

Outputs:

- normalized power-market DataFrame

Important practical detail:

The repository tries to fetch rich ENTSO-E context such as intraday prices,
imbalance prices, and intraday renewable forecast data, but these series are
not equally available for every zone and time range.

So in the final system, you should think of them like valuable bonus context
rather than guaranteed core inputs.

The pipeline still requires core market and weather information.
But if optional intraday or imbalance series are missing, the repository can
continue with neutral fallback logic and explicit availability flags instead of
crashing immediately.

Why this design was chosen:

The rest of the pipeline should not care about ENTSO-E response quirks.

Alternative:

Put API logic directly inside the pipeline.

Why that is worse:

That mixes acquisition logic with research logic and becomes harder to test or
debug.

-----------------------------
src/data_pipeline/ingest.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

This file coordinates market-data ingestion and weather ingestion.

It also creates synthetic fallback energy data when real ENTSO-E access fails.

Why it exists:

The project needs to remain runnable even without live market data, but it
must also preserve the research distinction between real and synthetic runs.

Inputs:

- AppConfig
- zone
- lookback window

Outputs:

- energy DataFrame
- weather DataFrame
- energy source label such as `entsoe` or `synthetic`

Why this design was chosen:

The pipeline can still be exercised in restricted environments, while the
output keeps a truth label showing whether the run is research-grade.

Tradeoff:

Synthetic fallback is useful for testing infrastructure, but dangerous if a
user forgets that synthetic success is not trading evidence.

-----------------------------
src/data_pipeline/clean.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

This file cleans market and weather data.

It handles:

- timestamp parsing
- numeric coercion
- interpolation
- short forward and backward filling
- column normalization for expanded market context

Why it exists:

Raw data is rarely clean enough for modeling.

Why this design matters:

Bad timestamps, missing values, and string-number issues can silently ruin
research results.

Alternative:

Drop every row with any issue.

Tradeoff:

That may produce a smaller, cleaner dataset, but also wastes information.
The current design allows mild repair while keeping research aware of data
quality through anomaly review.

-----------------------------
src/data_pipeline/merge.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

It merges cleaned energy and weather data on hourly timestamps.

Why it exists:

Market variables and weather variables come from different sources. They need
to be aligned into one modeling table.

Tradeoff:

An inner merge is simple and safe, but can drop timestamps when one side is
missing. That is acceptable here because consistent hourly alignment matters.

-----------------------------
src/data_pipeline/run_pipeline.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

It orchestrates ingest, clean, and merge.

Why it exists:

This creates a clean boundary between "get usable data" and "run research."

Inputs:

- AppConfig
- zone
- lookback window

Outputs:

- merged DataFrame
- energy_source tag

-----------------------------
src/features/build_features.py
-----------------------------

What it does:

This file transforms raw aligned data into model features.

Examples include:

- hour/day/month seasonality
- lagged values
- rolling means and standard deviations
- price ramps
- renewable penetration
- net load
- spread volatility
- imbalance z-scores
- interaction terms
- optional-context availability flags

Why it exists:

Raw columns are often too primitive.

A model may not automatically infer all useful structures from the base data,
especially in smaller datasets.

Why this design was chosen:

Hand-engineered features can encode domain intuition:

- timing matters
- spreads matter
- ramping matters
- volatility matters
- renewable forecast stress matters

Alternative:

Use raw time series only and let a deep model discover everything.

Tradeoff:

That can work in very large datasets, but this repository prefers a more
interpretable, domain-aware approach.

Another important final-product behavior is that missing optional ENTSO-E
context does not automatically kill the feature pipeline anymore.

If imbalance or intraday context is unavailable, the feature layer can fall
back to neutral proxy values while preserving flags that say whether the richer
context was genuinely available.

That makes the system more robust operationally while still being honest in the
research interpretation.

-----------------------------
src/models/*
-----------------------------

This folder contains model training logic.

Important files:

- `walk_forward.py`
- `model_registry.py`
- `train_xgb.py`
- `train_lstm.py`
- `train_prophet.py`
- `diagnostics.py`
- `base.py`

Let us explain them one by one.

src/models/walk_forward.py

What it does:

Creates chronological train/test windows.

Why it exists:

Trading research must respect time order.

Practical implication:

The model only trains on older data and is evaluated on future data.

There is one additional practical detail in the final version of the
repository.

Feature engineering uses lags and rolling windows, so it naturally removes some
early rows from the usable sample. Because of that, the workflow may end up
with slightly fewer rows than the raw history window suggests.

Instead of failing immediately in those borderline cases, the workflow can now
shrink the training window to the largest feasible size while keeping the test
window fixed, as long as there is still enough data for at least one honest
walk-forward split.

If there is not enough data even for one proper split, the repository still
raises an error. That is the correct behavior, because below that threshold the
evaluation would stop being meaningful.

src/models/model_registry.py

What it does:

Provides one place to map model names like `xgboost`, `lstm`, and `prophet`
to their training functions.

Why it exists:

This keeps orchestration simple and modular.

Alternative:

Hard-code branching logic everywhere.

Why that is worse:

Harder to maintain, harder to extend.

src/models/base.py

What it does:

Defines shared training output structure.

Why it exists:

Different model trainers should return outputs in a consistent format.

That makes downstream comparison, diagnostics, and artifact persistence much
easier.

src/models/train_xgb.py

What it does:

========================================================================
5. STEP-BY-STEP PIPELINE EXPLANATION
========================================================================

Now let us walk chronologically through the pipeline.

-----------------------------
STAGE 1: DATA INGESTION
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Get market and weather data into the system.

Inputs:

- ENTSO-E API access if available
- Open-Meteo weather data
- zone and lookback settings

Outputs:

- raw energy dataset
- raw weather dataset
- energy source label

Hidden assumptions:

- ENTSO-E data fields are sufficiently stable
- zone mappings are correct
- time alignment is meaningful at hourly resolution

What can go wrong:

- API failure
- missing credentials
- inconsistent market fields
- missing intraday or imbalance coverage

Why this approach is reasonable:

It separates collection from analysis and preserves a clear truth flag about
data quality level.

In the final implementation, missing intraday or imbalance coverage usually
creates a degraded-context run rather than an automatic hard stop, as long as
the required core market and weather fields are still usable.

-----------------------------
STAGE 2: DATA CLEANING
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Turn messy raw data into usable aligned data.

Inputs:

- raw market data
- raw weather data

Outputs:

- cleaned market data
- cleaned weather data

Hidden assumptions:

- short interpolation is acceptable
- missing values are not too extensive

What can go wrong:

- too much filling can hide real problems
- timestamps may misalign
- bad source data may survive light cleaning

Why reasonable:

Research needs usable data, but the anomaly review helps prevent blind trust.

-----------------------------
STAGE 3: FEATURE ENGINEERING
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Translate raw columns into patterns the models and trading logic can use.

Inputs:

- cleaned merged table

Outputs:

- feature-rich modeling table

Hidden assumptions:

- the engineered features actually capture useful economics
- rolling windows and lag structures are sensible

What can go wrong:

- noisy features
- redundant features
- features that look good in one regime but fail later

Why reasonable:

Power markets have structure. Encoding timing, imbalance, spread, and
volatility information is often better than hoping models discover all of it
unaided.

-----------------------------
STAGE 4: WALK-FORWARD TRAINING
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Train only on the past and test on future windows repeatedly.

Inputs:

- features table
- model choice

Outputs:

- out-of-sample predictions
- persisted final models
- training metrics
- diagnostics

Hidden assumptions:

- train and test windows are representative
- the retraining cadence is reasonable

What can go wrong:

- too little history
- unstable model behavior across folds
- concept drift

Why reasonable:

It is one of the strongest protections against fake backtest optimism.

In the final repository, this stage is also slightly more user-friendly than a
strict fixed-window implementation. If feature engineering shortens the sample
enough that the originally requested train window no longer fits cleanly, the
workflow can reduce the train window automatically instead of failing
immediately, provided there is still enough data for a valid chronological
split.

-----------------------------
STAGE 5: FORECAST GENERATION
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Produce predicted demand, renewables, and price.

Why all three?

Because price is not isolated. Demand and renewables help describe the
fundamental state that may drive price behavior.

What can go wrong:

- predictions may be smooth but miss important spikes
- forecast averages may look fine while stressed periods are poor

-----------------------------
STAGE 6: SIGNAL GENERATION
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Convert predictions and market context into position intent.

Inputs:

- model outputs
- current prices
- spread data
- rolling volatility
- net load and imbalance context

Outputs:

- signal-family scores
- regime labels
- target positions

Hidden assumptions:

- the chosen heuristics are directionally sensible
- regime detection is useful enough to matter

What can go wrong:

- too much signal mixing can dilute edge
- bad regime classification can hurt performance

Why reasonable:

Electricity markets are too complex for one raw predicted price delta to
capture everything.

-----------------------------
STAGE 7: POSITION SIZING
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Turn signal strength into actual exposure.

Inputs:

- combined signal
- volatility
- caps
- liquidity limits

Outputs:

- constrained position path

Hidden assumptions:

- smaller size in stressed regimes is safer
- caps avoid unrealistic leverage

What can go wrong:

- too much constraint can suppress good opportunities
- too little constraint can create fake or dangerous results

Why reasonable:

Real desks cannot size without limits.

-----------------------------
STAGE 8: BACKTESTING
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Simulate how the strategy would have performed.

Inputs:

- target positions
- market prices
- execution assumptions

Outputs:

- gross and net PnL
- costs
- equity curve
- drawdown
- attribution

Hidden assumptions:

- simplified spread and slippage models are still approximations
- hourly execution captures broad behavior but not full microstructure

What can go wrong:

- execution assumptions may still be too optimistic
- order book effects are missing

Why reasonable:

This is much better than a toy backtest and appropriate for a research stage.

-----------------------------
STAGE 9: BASELINE COMPARISON
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Check whether the strategy actually beats easy alternatives.

Why important:

Many sophisticated strategies are secretly no better than simple baselines.

Outputs:

- persistence results
- seasonal results
- naive mean-reversion results
- zero-signal results

-----------------------------
STAGE 10: BOOTSTRAP SIGNIFICANCE TESTING
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Measure whether apparent outperformance may just be luck.

Inputs:

- return streams from strategies

Outputs:

- Sharpe confidence intervals
- pairwise significance results

What can go wrong:

- short samples produce fragile inference
- significance is still conditional on the sample and assumptions

Why reasonable:

It is far better than reporting one point estimate and pretending certainty.

-----------------------------
STAGE 11: ANOMALY ANALYSIS
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Check whether data quality issues may distort research.

Outputs:

- anomaly review JSON

Why reasonable:

It helps avoid silent contamination from strange input behavior.

-----------------------------
STAGE 12: RESEARCH-NOTE GENERATION
-----------------------------

Purpose:

Translate quantitative outputs into a human research conclusion.

Outputs:

- structured summary JSON
- research note text

Why reasonable:

Trading research is not complete until a human can read and debate the
results.


========================================================================
6. FEATURE ENGINEERING MASTERCLASS
========================================================================

This is where we explain the major features in intuitive terms.

What is net load?

Definition:

Net load is demand minus renewable generation.

Simple intuition:

It tells you how much demand remains after renewables have done their part.

Why traders care:

If net load is high, the system may need more conventional generation, which
can support higher prices.

Example:

Demand = 50 GW
Renewables = 20 GW
Net load = 30 GW

If renewables suddenly fall to 10 GW, net load jumps to 40 GW.
That can stress the system and prices.

What is renewable forecast error?

Definition:

Difference between expected renewable output and realized renewable output, or
a proxy for that difference.

Intuition:

This measures how wrong the system may be about "free" or low-marginal-cost
power availability.

Why traders care:

Big renewable forecast misses can create sharp repricing.

What is rolling volatility?

Definition:

A moving estimate of how much prices have been fluctuating recently.

Intuition:

It measures how shaky the market has been.

Analogy:

If a road has been calm and straight, you can drive normally.
If it has been icy and chaotic, you slow down.

Why traders care:

High volatility often means:

- bigger risk
- wider spreads
- worse slippage
- more fragile signals

What are spread features?

Definition:

Features based on the difference between intraday and day-ahead prices.

Intuition:

These features measure short-term repricing pressure.

Why traders care:

If intraday is much richer than day-ahead, the market may be reacting to new
stress or surprise information.

What are imbalance spreads?

Definition:

Features based on the difference between imbalance prices and general market
prices.

Intuition:

They reflect how expensive real-time correction is becoming.

Why traders care:

This can reveal market stress that is not obvious from the main price alone.

What are lag features?

Definition:

Past values of the same variable, such as price one hour ago or 24 hours ago.

Intuition:

The recent past often contains information about the near future.

Why traders care:

Power markets often show short-term persistence, repeated intraday shapes, or
mean reversion from recent dislocations.

What are interaction terms?

Definition:

Features that combine two effects, such as net load multiplied by a time-of-day
seasonality term.

Intuition:

Sometimes a variable matters more in one context than another.

Example:

High net load at 3 AM may matter differently than high net load at 6 PM.

What is an equilibrium window?

Definition:

A rolling window used to estimate a "normal" reference level for prices.

Intuition:

If price is far away from its recent normal level, a mean-reversion signal may
argue that the move could partially unwind.

What are volatility regimes?

Definition:

States like high-volatility and low-volatility.

Why traders care:

The same raw signal may be more trustworthy or less tradable depending on the
regime.

Risks and limitations of features:

- a useful feature in one period may fail later
- too many features can encourage overfitting
- a plausible economic story does not guarantee stable edge
- some features may be correlated and partially redundant


========================================================================
7. MODELING SECTION
========================================================================

What is XGBoost?

Simple explanation:

XGBoost is a powerful method that combines many decision trees.

Each new tree tries to correct mistakes from earlier trees.

Analogy:

Imagine a team of tutors where each new tutor focuses on the mistakes the last
one missed.

Strengths:

- very strong on structured tabular data
- handles nonlinear relationships well
- often performs well with feature engineering
- relatively efficient
- provides feature importance

Weaknesses:

- may not naturally model sequence structure as elegantly as a dedicated
  sequence model
- can still overfit if pushed too hard

Why it may work in power markets:

Power data often has complex nonlinear relationships between weather, demand,
renewables, and price.

Why it may fail:

If the important structure depends heavily on longer temporal sequence patterns
or changing regimes, trees may miss part of the story.

What is LSTM?

Simple explanation:

LSTM is a type of neural network designed for sequences.

It tries to remember relevant past information while forgetting less useful
information.

Analogy:

Think of it like a note-taking assistant that decides what to keep in working
memory as events unfold over time.

Strengths:

- designed for sequential data
- can capture time dependencies

Weaknesses:

- slower and heavier
- more sensitive to tuning
- may underperform on smaller structured datasets
- harder to interpret

Why it may work in power markets:

Power markets are time series with evolving patterns.

Why it may fail:

If data volume is modest and handcrafted features already capture much of the
structure, LSTM may not justify its complexity.

What is Prophet?

Simple explanation:

Prophet is a forecasting method designed to handle trend and seasonality in a
clean, interpretable way.

Strengths:

- interpretable
- convenient for calendar structure
- useful when seasonality is important

Weaknesses:

- may be too rigid for very nonlinear short-term market behavior
- may not capture violent dislocations as well as more flexible methods

Why it may work in power markets:

Electricity has strong seasonal and intraday patterns.

Why it may fail:

Stress events are often nonlinear and abrupt.

Why is walk-forward validation critical?

Because time matters.

A random train/test split is dangerous in time series trading research because
it mixes future and past information in unnatural ways.

Why random splits can be dangerous:

If the model sees examples from the future during training, it may learn
patterns that would not be available in real life.

How leakage happens:

- using future statistics in feature construction
- random shuffling across time
- fitting scalers on the full dataset
- engineering seasonality averages using future rows

How leakage destroys trading research:

It gives fake confidence.
A leaking model can appear impressive in backtests and fail instantly in live
conditions.


========================================================================
8. SIGNAL GENERATION AND TRADING LOGIC
========================================================================

Now we move from "predicting" to "acting."

What is `forecast_signal`?

This measures the edge implied by the predicted future price relative to the
current price, scaled by volatility.

Intuition:

If the model expects price to move up meaningfully compared with normal noise,
the signal leans long.

What is `mean_reversion_signal`?

This measures whether current price has moved unusually far from a rolling
equilibrium level.

Intuition:

If price looks stretched relative to recent normal conditions, the market may
partially snap back.

Analogy:

Imagine a rubber band. If you pull it far from center, it may pull back.

What is `fundamental_signal`?

This uses demand-renewable imbalance and related market structure.

Intuition:

If the system appears tighter than normal, prices may have upward pressure.
If the system appears looser than normal, prices may have downward pressure.

Why multiple signal families?

Because markets are not driven by one mechanism only.

Sometimes price forecasts capture the move best.
Sometimes dislocations mean-revert.
Sometimes fundamentals dominate.

Why use `combined_signal`?

Because combining complementary signals can be more robust than relying on one
fragile idea.

What is `target_position`?

This is the strategy's desired exposure after signal processing and risk
controls.

Why volatility scaling?

Because a strong signal in a violent market may still deserve a smaller
position than an equally strong signal in a calmer market.

Why position caps?

Because unconstrained sizing can create unrealistic and dangerous exposure.

Why liquidity caps?

Because even if the model wants to flip instantly from strongly short to
strongly long, real trading may not allow that cleanly.

Trend following versus mean reversion:

Trend following says:

"If the move has started, maybe it continues."

Mean reversion says:

"If the move has gone too far too fast, maybe it snaps back."

Both ideas can be valid in different regimes.

That is why regime switching exists.

Equilibrium pricing:

The equilibrium idea is simply that markets often have a moving "normal"
center. Large departures from that center may contain information.


========================================================================
9. BACKTESTING MASTERCLASS
========================================================================

What is a backtest?

A backtest is a simulation of how a strategy would have behaved historically
under a chosen set of assumptions.

Why naive backtests are misleading:

Because they often assume away the hardest parts of trading.

Examples of naive assumptions:

- you trade instantly at the observed price
- there is no spread
- there is no slippage
- you can size however you want
- you never disturb the market

That is not serious enough for trading research.

What is spread cost?

The spread is the gap between the best buying price and best selling price.

If you buy, you often pay the ask.
If you sell, you often receive the bid.

That gap is a real cost.

Why it matters:

Small forecast edges can disappear once spread is included.

What is slippage?

Slippage is getting a worse fill than you hoped for.

Why does it happen?

- market moves while you execute
- liquidity is thin
- urgency is high

What is execution delay?

The strategy acts one step later than the signal generation moment.

Why it matters:

You cannot use information at time t to pretend you traded at time t before
the market reacted.

What is turnover?

How much the position changes from one step to the next.

Why does turnover matter?

Every change can trigger costs.

What is a liquidity constraint?

It limits how much the strategy can change its position per step.

Why it matters:

This prevents magical trading behavior.

What is gross PnL versus net PnL?

Gross PnL:

Profit before costs.

Net PnL:

Profit after costs.

Why this difference matters:

Many fake trading projects proudly show gross PnL and quietly ignore how much
net PnL shrinks after realistic frictions.

Why many ML trading projects are fake:

Because they:

- use future information
- ignore spread
- ignore slippage
- ignore execution delay
- ignore baseline comparison
- optimize too aggressively on one backtest

How this repository tries to avoid fake results:

- walk-forward validation
- delayed execution
- spread and slippage modeling
- position and liquidity constraints
- baseline comparison
- significance testing
- explicit limitation reporting

What remains unrealistic?

- no full order book model
- no true market impact model
- no live latency simulation
- no portfolio interactions
- simplified cost assumptions


========================================================================
10. STATISTICS AND SIGNIFICANCE
========================================================================

Why is Sharpe ratio useful?

Because raw profit alone can be misleading.

If Strategy A makes EUR 1000 with wild instability and Strategy B makes
EUR 900 more steadily, Strategy B may be more attractive.

What is a confidence interval?

Simple definition:

A range that shows plausible uncertainty around an estimate.

Why it matters:

A Sharpe ratio of 1.0 looks nice, but if uncertainty is huge, confidence in
the result should be limited.

What is bootstrap testing?

Bootstrap means repeatedly resampling historical returns to estimate how
unstable a metric might be.

Analogy:

Imagine shuffling and re-sampling observations many times to ask:

"If history were slightly rearranged, would this result still look strong?"

Why is significance testing important?

Because a positive result may simply be lucky.

Why small samples are dangerous:

Short histories can make random success look like skill.

Why positive Sharpe is not enough:

Because:

- it may be driven by one regime
- it may not beat baselines meaningfully
- it may not survive cost changes
- confidence bounds may be weak

Why benchmark comparison matters:

If your fancy model does not beat persistence or a simple seasonal average,
the fancy model may not add real value.

Why persistence baselines matter:

Because markets often contain inertia.

Sometimes "tomorrow looks like today" is harder to beat than beginners expect.

In this repository, the LLM helps with:

- anomaly summarization
- research-note summarization

It does not own the numerical truth.


========================================================================
12. REAL-WORLD LIMITATIONS
========================================================================

This section must be brutally honest.

This is still research infrastructure.

That means:

- it is a tool for asking good questions
- it is not proof of durable alpha
- it is not a production trading stack

Why production trading is much harder:

Because real trading desks need much more than signal research.

Missing portfolio optimization:

A real desk usually trades across assets, regions, products, and time buckets.
This repository is single-stream.

Missing order-book modeling:

Real fills depend on market depth, queue position, and order type.
This repository uses simplified execution approximations.

Missing latency modeling:

Real systems have delays in:

- data arrival
- compute
- order routing
- exchange acknowledgment

Missing cross-market optimization:

Professional desks may exploit relationships across:

- neighboring countries
- fuel markets
- emissions markets
- ancillary markets
- spreads across delivery periods

Missing proper risk engine:

Institutional systems track:

- stress scenarios
- concentration limits
- VaR-like metrics
- exposure aggregation
- kill switches

Missing uncertainty-aware sizing:

The current system uses heuristic signal scaling.
A stronger system might size based on calibrated uncertainty.

Synthetic fallback dangers:

Synthetic data is useful for infrastructure testing, but dangerous if users
forget that synthetic success is not market success.

Partial synthetic regions require caution too.

A run can still be operationally useful if only a small fraction of rows are
synthetic or partially synthetic, but the contamination must remain visible in
research interpretation.

Parameter instability:

A parameter that looks good today may degrade tomorrow.

Regime shifts:

Power markets change because of:

- weather
- regulation
- interconnector availability
- generation mix changes
- storage expansion
- demand shifts

Non-stationarity:

This is the formal way of saying the world changes over time.

Patterns do not stay fixed forever.

How real trading desks operate differently:

They usually require:

- stronger data infrastructure
- lower-latency systems
- richer execution modeling
- portfolio coordination
- governance
- monitoring
- auditability
- trader oversight
- production deployment controls

========================================================================
14. GLOSSARY
========================================================================

Alpha
Simple: A useful trading edge.
Technical: Return attributable to skill rather than passive exposure or luck.
Why it matters: Without alpha, there is no reason to trade.

Ancillary Services
Simple: Support services that keep the grid stable.
Technical: Reserve and balancing-related services supporting frequency and reliability.
Why it matters: They influence system stress and flexible asset value.

Anomaly
Simple: Something strange in the data.
Technical: An unusual observation or structural deviation from expected behavior.
Why it matters: Bad anomalies can ruin research conclusions.

Backtest
Simple: A historical trading simulation.
Technical: A rules-based reconstruction of how a strategy would have behaved under stated assumptions.
Why it matters: It is the main research testing environment.

Baseline
Simple: A simple strategy used for comparison.
Technical: A benchmark model or trading rule used to judge whether a proposed strategy adds value.
Why it matters: Fancy systems must beat simple alternatives.

Bid/Ask Spread
Simple: The gap between buy and sell prices.
Technical: The difference between the best offer and the best bid.
Why it matters: It is a real trading cost.

Bootstrap
Simple: Re-sampling history many times to estimate uncertainty.
Technical: A nonparametric resampling method used to estimate the distribution of a statistic.
Why it matters: It helps show whether results may be fragile.

Combined Signal
Simple: The final mixture of several trading clues.
Technical: Weighted aggregation of forecast, mean-reversion, and fundamental signals.
Why it matters: It drives the strategy's position intent.

Day-Ahead Market
Simple: The market for planning tomorrow's electricity.
Technical: Forward hourly power market cleared before delivery day.
Why it matters: It anchors expected supply-demand conditions.

Deterministic Fallback
Simple: A rules-based backup when the LLM is unavailable.
Technical: Non-LLM logic that preserves output schema and operational continuity.
Why it matters: Keeps the research workflow reliable.

Drawdown
Simple: A fall from a previous peak.
Technical: Peak-to-trough decline in cumulative equity.
Why it matters: Large drawdowns can make a strategy unacceptable.

Execution Delay
Simple: Acting later than the signal is calculated.
Technical: Shifting position implementation forward in time to reduce lookahead bias and mimic real reaction lag.
Why it matters: Instant execution assumptions are often fake.

Feature
Simple: A useful input variable for a model.
Technical: A transformed explanatory variable used by predictive or signal models.
Why it matters: Good features can encode economic insight.

Forecast Signal
Simple: A signal based on predicted price movement.
Technical: Volatility-scaled expression of model-implied future price edge.
Why it matters: It converts forecast information into tradable intensity.

Fundamental Signal
Simple: A signal based on real system conditions like net load.
Technical: A structured signal derived from demand-renewable imbalance and related context.
Why it matters: Price often reflects physical system tightness.

Gross PnL
Simple: Profit before costs.
Technical: Raw profit from price movement before execution and transaction charges.
Why it matters: Gross results can look good while net results fail.

Imbalance Market
Simple: The market for fixing real-time mismatches.
Technical: Settlement and pricing layer for deviations between scheduled and realized power positions.
Why it matters: It can reflect system stress sharply.

Intraday Market
Simple: The market for adjusting closer to real time.
Technical: Short-horizon electricity market used after day-ahead clearing and before delivery.
Why it matters: New information arrives and reprices risk.

Leakage
Simple: Accidentally using future information.
Technical: Contamination of training or evaluation with data unavailable at decision time.
Why it matters: Leakage creates fake strategy performance.

Liquidity Constraint
Simple: A limit on how fast you can change position.
Technical: A maximum allowed position change per step reflecting execution realism.
Why it matters: Prevents unrealistic instant flips.

LSTM
Simple: A neural network designed for sequences.
Technical: Long Short-Term Memory architecture for temporal modeling.
Why it matters: It may capture time dependencies that trees miss.

Market Coupling
Simple: Linking neighboring power markets.
Technical: Coordinated cross-border market clearing using transmission capacity.
Why it matters: Prices across regions influence each other.

Market Regime
Simple: The current style of market behavior.
Technical: A latent or defined state such as trend, mean reversion, calm, or stressed.
Why it matters: Strategy behavior may need to change across regimes.

Mean Reversion
Simple: The idea that stretched prices may move back toward normal.
Technical: A trading hypothesis that deviations from equilibrium partially unwind over time.
Why it matters: Common in temporary dislocation periods.

Net Load
Simple: Demand left over after renewables.
Technical: Demand minus renewable generation.
Why it matters: A key driver of system tightness and price pressure.

Non-Stationarity
Simple: The world changes over time.
Technical: Statistical properties of the process are not constant over time.
Why it matters: Models can decay as regimes shift.

Overfitting
Simple: Memorizing the past instead of learning useful patterns.
Technical: Excess adaptation to sample-specific noise.
Why it matters: Overfit models often fail out of sample.

PnL
Simple: Profit and loss.
Technical: Net or gross financial outcome from trading activity.
Why it matters: It is what trading research ultimately tries to explain.

Position
Simple: Your market exposure.
Technical: The signed amount of long or short risk the strategy holds.
Why it matters: Signals matter only if translated into positions.

Position Cap
Simple: A maximum allowed exposure.
Technical: Hard bound on position magnitude.
Why it matters: Limits unrealistic or dangerous sizing.

Prophet
Simple: A forecasting model focused on seasonality and trend.
Technical: A decomposable time-series model with trend, seasonality, and regressor support.
Why it matters: Useful as an interpretable forecasting alternative.

Research Grade
Simple: Serious enough for honest research, not just a toy demo.
Technical: A workflow that includes anti-leakage validation, realistic backtest elements, baseline comparison, and transparent limitations.
Why it matters: Separates disciplined research from weak storytelling.

RMSE
Simple: A measure of average prediction error size.
Technical: Root Mean Squared Error.
Why it matters: Useful, but not enough for trading evaluation.

Seasonal Baseline
Simple: A simple forecast based on repeating hour/day patterns.
Technical: Prediction using historical hour-of-day and day-of-week averages.
Why it matters: Harder to beat than many beginners expect.

Sharpe Ratio
Simple: Reward per unit of risk.
Technical: Mean return divided by return volatility, often annualized.
Why it matters: Helps judge risk-adjusted performance.

Signal
Simple: A clue used for trading.
Technical: A numerical or logical indicator used to guide exposure decisions.
Why it matters: It connects data to action.

Slippage
Simple: Paying a worse price than expected.
Technical: Execution shortfall relative to the reference price.
Why it matters: It reduces real profitability.

Synthetic Fallback
Simple: Artificial data used when real market data fails.
Technical: Simulated energy-market-like data used to keep infrastructure operational.
Why it matters: Helpful for testing, dangerous for real performance claims.

Target Position
Simple: The position the strategy wants to hold.
Technical: The desired exposure after signal transformation and sizing logic.
Why it matters: It is the bridge between signal and executed exposure.

Turnover
Simple: How much you keep changing your position.
Technical: Absolute position change over time.
Why it matters: More turnover usually means more cost.

Volatility
Simple: How violently prices move.
Technical: Statistical dispersion or variability of returns or price changes.
Why it matters: It affects risk, sizing, and execution quality.

Volatility Scaling
Simple: Shrinking positions when the market is unstable.
Technical: Position adjustment inversely related to recent volatility.
Why it matters: Helps manage risk in stressed conditions.

Walk-Forward Validation
Simple: Train on the past, test on the next future block, and repeat.
Technical: Rolling or staged chronological out-of-sample evaluation.
Why it matters: Essential for honest trading research.

XGBoost
Simple: A strong tree-based ML model.
Technical: Gradient-boosted decision tree ensemble.
Why it matters: Often a powerful benchmark for structured data.


========================================================================
15. FINAL MENTAL MODEL
========================================================================

Let us end by telling the whole story in one continuous narrative.

Imagine you join an energy trading desk as a junior quant researcher.

Every day, the desk faces a hard problem:

Electricity supply and demand must stay balanced.
Weather changes constantly.
Renewable output is uncertain.
Prices in day-ahead, intraday, and imbalance layers react to new information.

Your job is not just to build a model that predicts something.

Your job is to ask:

"Can I use data to form a view about future market behavior that is strong
enough to survive real-world trading friction?"

So the repository begins by collecting market and weather data.

It keeps that raw history in canonical parquet caches for each source and
zone, validates those caches carefully, and fetches only the missing hourly
ranges for the next run.

It tries to understand:

- what day-ahead prices said
- what intraday prices changed to
- whether imbalance conditions looked stressed
- how demand and renewables evolved
- what the weather was doing

Then it cleans that data, because raw data is noisy and can betray you.
It may interpolate small bounded gaps, but it keeps provenance visible so
cleaning does not magically turn synthetic regions into real ones.

Then it engineers features, because raw numbers alone do not explain the market
well enough.

It builds things like net load, spread pressure, volatility, seasonality, and
renewable stress indicators.

Then it trains models using walk-forward logic so that the future stays hidden
until it is time to test.

Those models predict:

- demand
- renewables
- price

But the system does not stop there.

Because prediction is not yet trading.

The repository then asks:

- does the model imply price edge?
- is price stretched away from equilibrium?
- do fundamentals look tight or loose?
- is the market in a trend regime or mean-reversion regime?
- is volatility calm or dangerous?

It translates all of that into signal families:

- forecast signal
- mean reversion signal
- fundamental signal

Then it combines them into a target position, while shrinking or capping that
position when the market looks more dangerous.

Then comes the honesty test:

Could this strategy still look good after we assume delayed execution, spread,
slippage, turnover costs, and liquidity limits?

That is what the backtest tries to answer.

Then comes the humility test:

Did this sophisticated strategy actually beat:

- persistence
- seasonal repetition
- a naive mean-reversion rule
- doing nothing

If not, then the sophistication may not be worth much.

Then comes the statistics test:

Is the outperformance stable enough to be interesting, or could it easily be
luck?

That is why bootstrap confidence intervals and significance comparisons exist.

Then comes the judgment test:

Did the data look clean?
Were there strange spikes or breaks?
Did one model look best for trading, not just for RMSE?
Did the edge appear in all regimes or only a few?

Finally, the repository writes a research note.

That note is the adult conclusion of the whole pipeline.

It says, in effect:

"Here is the market idea.
Here is why it might work.
Here is how we tested it.
Here is what it beat.
Here is what it failed to beat.
Here is how strong or weak the evidence is.
Here is what still worries us.
Here is what we would do next."

If you understand that full story, then you understand this repository.

You understand that it is not a toy forecasting demo.
You understand that it is not a magical black-box money machine.
You understand that it is a serious apprenticeship environment for learning
how modern data science, market structure, trading logic, and honest
backtesting connect inside power-market research.

That is the correct mental model.

Raw market data becomes cleaned data.
Cleaned data becomes features.
Features become forecasts.
Forecasts plus structural market logic become signals.
Signals become constrained positions.
Positions become simulated trades.
Trades become gross and net PnL.
PnL is judged against risk, costs, baselines, and uncertainty.
Those judgments become research conclusions.

That is the full chain.

If you can explain that chain clearly, explain why each step exists, explain
where the system is strong, explain where it remains simplified, and explain
why trading research must be harsher than ordinary ML evaluation, then you are
already thinking like a junior quant researcher rather than a beginner.
